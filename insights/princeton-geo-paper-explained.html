The Princeton GEO paper, explained for Australian business owners

In 2024, researchers from Princeton University, Georgia Tech, IIT Delhi, and the Allen Institute for AI published the first peer-reviewed study on how to get your website cited by AI tools like ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google's AI Overviews. They tested nine tactics across 10,000 real queries. Three of them — adding quotations, including statistics, and citing credible sources — boosted visibility in AI answers by 28–41%, with quotations as the standout performer. One widely used tactic, keyword stuffing, actively hurt. And the finding almost nobody talks about: the smaller your website, the bigger the gain.

What is the Princeton GEO paper?

GEO: Generative Engine Optimization (Aggarwal et al., 2024) was presented at KDD, one of the world's top data-science conferences. The researchers coined the term "generative engine" for tools like ChatGPT and Perplexity that answer questions by synthesising multiple sources — rather than handing you ten blue links.

Their problem statement is the one every business owner now faces: when an AI gives one answer instead of ten links, who gets seen? The paper built a testing framework called GEO-bench, comprising 10,000 queries across 8 domains, and measured how content changes affect whether — and how prominently — a site gets cited in the AI's answer.

This matters because it is the only foundation in this industry that is not vendor marketing. Every credible AEO tool and agency anchors to this study. It introduced two metrics that have become the standard for measuring AI citations: Position Adjusted Word Count (how many words from your source appear in the AI's answer, weighted by position) and Subjective Impression (overall visibility within the response).

What they tested: 9 tactics, 3 clear winners

The researchers rewrote website content nine different ways and measured the change in AI visibility on a generative engine designed to closely mimic Bing Chat. They then validated the strongest findings on live Perplexity.ai, where top tactics produced a 37% real-world improvement in citations.

Two things stand out from the results.

First, the winners are all credibility signals. AI engines are trained to prefer content that demonstrates its claims — a hard number, a named source, a direct quote. The single biggest lever is showing your evidence, not repeating your keywords.

Second, the classic SEO playbook can backfire. Keyword stuffing — still standard practice at plenty of agencies — scored 17.8 against a baseline of 19.5: worse than no optimisation at all. If your current provider is optimising your site the 2015 way, you may be paying to go backwards in AI search.

"Cite Sources, Quotation Addition, and Statistics Addition emerge as consistently effective strategies, achieving a relative improvement of 30–40% on the Position-Adjusted Word Count metric."— Aggarwal, P., Murahari, V., et al., GEO: Generative Engine Optimization, KDD 2024 (arXiv:2311.09735)

The paper's most important finding for small businesses: GEO tactics delivered their largest relative gains to lower-ranked sites. With the Cite Sources tactic alone, websites ranked fifth saw a 115.1% increase in AI visibility — more than doubling their citation rate. Top-ranked sites saw a −30.3% effect from the same tactic. AI search is a leveller, not a reinforcer of existing rankings.

Why this matters for Australian SMBs specifically

In traditional Google search, position five might as well be invisible — the top result captures most clicks. The Princeton team's finding is that this logic does not carry over to AI search. AI engines synthesise a single answer from multiple sources. A Parramatta accountant or a Geelong manufacturer with well-evidenced content can out-cite a national brand on the queries that matter to them.

That window will not stay open forever. In the US, enterprise brands are already paying US$499+ per month for AI visibility tooling. Globally, 70% of organisations say AI search will reshape their strategy within three years, but only 20% have started acting on it (HubSpot, 2026). Most Australian businesses have not started.

The gap is widest right now. The businesses that move first — with evidence-backed content, external citations, and real quotations — will be the ones AI engines reach for when your customers ask for a recommendation.

What to actually do: a 5-step starting plan

  1. Audit one money page. Pick the page that wins you business. Count the verifiable claims on it. If it says "trusted by hundreds" instead of "trusted by 214 Australian builders since 2019", you have found your first fix.
  2. Cite credible sources. Link your factual claims to the Australian Bureau of Statistics, industry bodies, standards bodies (AS/NZS), or published research. The study recorded a +27.5% lift — solid third of the three winning tactics.
  3. Add statistics. Replace every vague claim with a real number — project counts, years in business, percentages, dollar figures. The study recorded a +30.6% lift, second strongest of the three.
  4. Add quotations. Customer quotes with names and businesses, or expert commentary. This was the single strongest tactic in the study — a 40.9% lift. AI engines lift well-attributed quotes into answers — make sure they are yours, not your competitor's.
  5. Stop keyword stuffing. If a paragraph reads like it was written for a robot, rewrite it for a person. The research says the robot prefers it too — keyword-stuffed content scored 17.8 against a baseline of 19.5.

Do this on one page, wait three to four weeks, then ask ChatGPT and Perplexity the questions your customers ask. Check whether you appear. That is a measurable, evidence-based start — no subscription required.

GEO tactics are quick wins — not a complete strategy

The Princeton study measures content signals in isolation: what you put on individual pages. Apply the three winning tactics and you will see real improvement in 3–8 weeks. That is exactly what the research says, and it is where every business should start.

What the study cannot measure is structural AI visibility — whether AI engines know your brand well enough to recommend you consistently, how clearly they understand your entity (who you are, what you do, where you operate), and whether your schema and citation signals back that up across hundreds of different queries. Those are not content fixes. They are architecture.

Frequently asked questions

Is GEO the same as AEO?
Near enough. GEO (generative engine optimisation) is the academic term from the Princeton paper; AEO (answer engine optimisation) is the more common industry term in Australia. Both mean optimising to be found, cited, and recommended in AI-generated answers.
Does GEO replace SEO?
No — it sits on top of it. AI engines still draw from search indexes, so technical SEO basics still matter. But the content rules differ significantly, and one legacy SEO habit — keyword stuffing — actively hurts your AI citation rate.
How long does it take to see results from GEO tactics?
AI engines refresh their source data on different cycles. Expect three to eight weeks before content changes show up in answers. The Princeton paper measured immediate effects in controlled tests; live engines lag. Apply the tactics, then check your AI visibility in four to six weeks.
Are GEO tactics enough on their own?
They are an excellent starting point — and most businesses haven't even done this much yet. Adding statistics and quotations to existing pages is fast — often a day or two of work. Sourcing and linking credible external citations takes a little longer depending on your industry, but all three tactics are achievable without specialist help and typically show results in 3–8 weeks. What they don't address is structural AI visibility: whether AI engines have a clear, consistent understanding of your brand, entity signals, and authority across many queries. That's what the AEO Traction Stack covers — it's the long-term framework GEO tactics plug into. Start with GEO quick wins. Build toward the Traction Stack.
Do these tactics work the same for every industry?
No — the paper explicitly found effectiveness varies by domain. Statistics worked hardest for factual, legal, and finance topics. Authoritative tone helped opinion-led topics. Quotations performed consistently across domains. This is why generic AEO advice underdelivers and domain-specific optimisation matters.
Where can I read the original paper?
It is free: arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735 — GEO: Generative Engine Optimization, Aggarwal et al., Proceedings of the 30th ACM SIGKDD Conference, 2024.
