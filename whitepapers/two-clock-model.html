Abstract

A structural AEO score measures whether a website can be read by machines. It does not measure whether AI platforms have updated what they say about the business behind that website. This paper develops a framework — the Two-Clock Model — that separates those two measurements and names the space between them the Perception Gap, formalised as G(t) = S(t) − P(t). Drawing on eighteen external studies spanning knowledge cutoffs, temporal misalignment, fact duration, retrieval-index staleness, long-tail knowledge, knowledge editing, and AI-search visibility measurement, we argue the gap is a structural property of how large language models and retrieval systems are built, not evidence that optimisation work has failed. An observed case (AISearch Global's own brand, "Client Zero") shows the pattern the framework predicts: a structural score moving from 19 at launch (20 May 2026) to 91 in under seven weeks, while AI perception scores across ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini stood at 36, 30, and 41 when measured one month in; the remainder of the twelve-month trajectory is a modelled projection, and the observed portion is so far consistent with the theory's prediction that the two clocks move independently. We identify the window in which the gap is typically widest (months 1.5–3), propose a month-six diagnostic threshold for telling an expected lag apart from a genuine problem, and set out three levers that narrow the gap over time: citation building, external mentions, and answer-format content.

Keywords: answer engine optimisation (AEO), generative engine optimisation (GEO), knowledge cutoffs, temporal misalignment, retrieval freshness, AI visibility, long-tail knowledge, Perception Gap

1. Introduction

A business runs an AEO program. Its structural score climbs from the teens into the nineties within weeks. Then someone asks ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Gemini about that business, and the model either says nothing or gets it wrong.1 Both failure modes are now documented at scale: mapping AI visibility across 100 B2B companies, Varga (2026) identifies an invisibility gap for small entities alongside a brand hallucination paradox, in which better-known Tier 1 brands drew fabricated citations more often than obscure Tier 3 entities (52.69% versus 37.87%). The natural conclusion, in either case, is that the AEO work didn't take. That conclusion is usually wrong, and it's wrong because it treats two separate questions as one.

The thesis of this paper can be stated in one sentence: the Perception Gap is an expected property of current AI architectures, not evidence of failed optimisation.

The first question is whether a site is machine-readable: schema in place, entities defined, content structured so a crawler or retriever can parse it. The second question is whether an AI platform has actually updated what it knows and says about the business. Most AEO reporting only ever answers the first question, because it's the one that's easy to measure and moves on a timeline the practitioner controls. The second question is harder, slower, and governed by systems no agency or business can directly operate. It is also the question with the higher stakes: comparing AI search against traditional search across 24,000 queries, Aral, Li, and Zuo (2026) find that AI search surfaces significantly fewer long-tail sources and lower response variety, concentrating visibility among fewer publishers. In a more concentrated visibility market, failing to register in the answer costs more than failing to rank on a results page did.

This paper is about the space between those two answers — what we call the Perception Gap. It is not a general survey of AEO tactics. It is an explanation of one specific, underexamined mechanism: why structural progress and AI recognition move at different speeds, what research into how language models actually work tells us about why that happens, and how to tell a normal lag apart from a genuine failure.

This paper makes three contributions:

  • First, we assemble five mechanisms from published research — on knowledge cutoffs, temporal misalignment, fact duration, retrieval-system design, and long-tail knowledge representation (labelled M1–M5 throughout) — that each independently predict a lag between structural optimisation and AI recognition, and show they describe the same underlying phenomenon.
  • Second, we formalise that phenomenon as the Two-Clock Model: a Structural Clock and a Perception Clock, moving at different speeds under different control, producing a measurable Perception Gap G(t) = S(t) − P(t).
  • Third, we illustrate the model against observed implementation data from a live case (Client Zero) and set out three evidence-based levers — citation building, external mentions, and answer-format content — for narrowing the gap, along with a diagnostic for distinguishing an expected lag from a genuine failure.

2. Theoretical Background: Why Machines Don't Update on Your Schedule

Five separate strands of research point at the same conclusion from different angles: the systems that decide what an AI platform says about a business do not, and structurally cannot, move at the speed a website can be edited. We label these mechanisms M1–M5 and use the labels throughout. Two further findings (Sections 2.6 and 2.7) bound the framework on either side: structural optimisation demonstrably works, and even achieved visibility is not a fixed state.

2.1 M1 — Knowledge cutoffs are not a clean line

AI providers publish a knowledge cutoff date alongside each model release, and it's treated as a hard boundary. Cheng et al. (2024) show it isn't one. Their analysis of open pretraining datasets finds that a model's demonstrated knowledge for a given topic or resource — what they term its effective cutoff — often diverges from its stated cutoff, for two reasons: older material persists inside newer training data dumps than the dump dates suggest, and deduplication processes that remove near-identical text can inadvertently strip out or distort recently updated pages. The practical result is that "the model was trained up to [date]" tells you far less than it appears to about what the model actually knows, or when, about a specific entity.

2.2 M2 — Models default to older knowledge even when newer knowledge exists

Zhao et al. (2024) tested this directly. Using 20,000 time-sensitive questions spanning 2000–2023, they found that models such as LLaMA2 — despite a pretraining cutoff in 2022 — mostly answered as though their knowledge stopped around 2019. Deliberately realigning the model to its actual cutoff year improved answer accuracy by up to 62%, but that realignment is a research intervention, not a feature switched on inside consumer products. In practice, a model's internal sense of "now" runs behind its stated cutoff by default. For a business whose most relevant activity — new positioning, new offers, recent coverage — happened in the last year, that default lag matters more than the headline cutoff date.

2.3 M3 — Facts decay at different rates, and the model doesn't sort them

Zhang and Choi (2023) introduce the idea of fact duration: some facts are stable for years, others change within months, and a model has no built-in way to tell which is which. A business's name and industry category are durable facts. Its current positioning, differentiators, and recent work are volatile facts — and volatile facts are exactly what determines whether a model recommends one business over a competitor. The category of information that matters most for AI visibility is the category most prone to staying stale in a model's memory.

2.4 M4 — Retrieval doesn't solve this on its own

Modern AI search products increasingly ground answers in retrieval — pulling from an index rather than relying purely on trained-in memory. That helps, but it isn't real-time. Research on retrieval system design describes a persistent staleness–latency–cost tradeoff, and the systems literature now documents it directly: work on freshness-aware retrieval indexing describes production pipelines built on periodic refresh and TTL-based caching, which inevitably creates stale windows between updates — and, as Li, Cao, Liu, and Duprey (2026) put it, "stale retrieval can directly surface as stale, confidently phrased answers even when the underlying model is unchanged." Benchmarking across index-update policies shows the same thing from the systems side: updates ignored until the next rebuild leave the pipeline retrieving stale data at degraded accuracy (S. Li et al., 2026). Nor does retrieval fully repair the temporal lag of M2: Vu et al. (2023), benchmarking models on fast-changing world knowledge, find that all tested LLMs struggle with it and that search-engine augmentation helps substantially without fully closing the gap. The index behind an AI platform's answer updates on its own cadence — not the moment a business publishes something new.

2.5 M5 — New and smaller entities carry the gap longest

The long tail is one of the most robust findings in the study of language-model knowledge. Kandpal, Deng, Roberts, Wallace, and Raffel (2023) show that a model's factual accuracy about an entity scales with the number of pre-training documents that mention that entity — and that scaling up the model does not fix the tail. Mallen et al. (2023) find the complementary pattern on 14,000 questions about entities of varying popularity: models fail disproportionately on low-popularity entities, and retrieval augmentation helps most exactly there. This links M4 to M5 — what sits in the retrieval index is the main channel through which a long-tail business gets surfaced at all.

Badhe, Shah, and Kathrotia (2026) describe the training-side mechanics: long-tail knowledge — information that's sparse, low-frequency, or newly formed relative to a model's training data — is disproportionately lost to mechanisms like gradient dilution and representational interference during training. A business three months into an AEO program is, from a model's point of view, exactly this kind of long-tail entity: thin, recent, under-represented relative to larger or longer-established competitors. That isn't a failure of the AEO work. It's a description of where a young or small brand structurally sits in a model's knowledge, until enough external signal accumulates to move it out of the tail.

Field measurement of AI search confirms the tiering at production scale. Analysing more than 100,000 prompt responses across more than 100 brands between March and May 2026, Kumar (2026) finds that household-name brands appear in 73% of relevant AI answers, mid-market brands in 44%, and niche or small brands in only 11% — a drop of roughly thirty percentage points per tier (Figure 1).

2.6 Structural optimisation is real, and it works — on its own axis

None of this means structural work is wasted. Aggarwal et al.'s GEO paper (2024), presented at KDD, is the strongest available evidence that content-level interventions — adding citations, statistics, quotations, and clearer structure — can lift a page's visibility inside generative engine responses by up to 40% in controlled benchmark testing. That's the fast clock, empirically confirmed. What the GEO paper doesn't claim, because it isn't what it measures, is how quickly any specific production model updates its live output for any specific business after those changes are made. That's the slow clock's territory, and it's a different question.

2.7 Even "visible" isn't a fixed state

Schulte, Bleeker, and Kaufmann (2026) measured how stable AI search answers are across repeated identical queries. Cited-source overlap between separate runs of the same query was only 32–43%. Day-to-day brand overlap averaged 45–59% (Figure 2). Their conclusion: AI search visibility is a distribution, not a single-point outcome, and a one-off check is not a reliable read on it. Kumar (2026) documents a second layer of the same instability: across his response panel, the sentiment framing of a brand flips roughly 6.7 times more often than whether the brand is mentioned at all — a business that is reliably surfaced is still not reliably characterised. This is a third reason the Perception Clock looks slow and uneven rather than a clean line climbing upward — because even where a business is being surfaced, that surfacing is probabilistic, not a switch that stays flipped.

3. The Two-Clock Model

Put together, mechanisms M1–M5 describe two independent systems running in parallel whenever AEO work is done. They don't run at the same speed, and only one of them takes direction from the business doing the work. Figure 3 states the model; Table 1 details the two clocks.

The Structural Clock is the one every AEO checklist measures, because it's the one that responds directly to work. Schema goes in, signals fire, the score moves — visibly, often daily. The Perception Clock is different in kind, not just speed. Publishing does not produce an instant update in what a model knows or says, because that update depends on cycles the business doesn't operate: when a model was last trained (M1, M2), how the model weights old against new facts (M3), when a retrieval index was last refreshed (M4), and how much third-party citation has accumulated in the meantime (M5).

4. The Perception Gap

The Perception Gap is the space between a high structural score and low, absent, or inaccurate AI recognition. It is the central concept in this paper, because it's the point where most businesses — and most agencies — misdiagnose what's actually happening.

4.1 Definition

Let S(t) denote a business's structural AEO score at time t, and let P(t) denote its AI perception score at time t, measured across the platforms it's being tracked against (where multiple platforms are tracked, P(t) may be read as the vector of platform scores or their average). The Perception Gap is:

G(t) = S(t) − P(t)

Two properties follow from the model. First, because S(t) responds to direct work while P(t) responds to platform-side cycles, an intensive structural program mechanically widens G(t) before anything can narrow it: S(t) rises in weeks while P(t) is still governed by M1–M5. Second, G(t) is not a single number so much as a widening-then-narrowing curve over the life of an engagement — steep early growth in S(t), a plateau, and then slow convergence as P(t) catches up.

4.2 Why the gap is the expected state, not a failure

Every mechanism reviewed in Section 2 points the same direction. Stated cutoffs don't match effective cutoffs (M1; Cheng et al., 2024). Models default to knowledge older than even their stated cutoff (M2; Zhao et al., 2024). The facts that matter most for AI recommendation are the ones most prone to staying stale (M3; Zhang & Choi, 2023). Retrieval indexes refresh on a schedule, not on demand (M4). New or small entities sit in the long tail of a model's knowledge until enough external signal pulls them out (M5; Kandpal et al., 2023; Badhe, Shah & Kathrotia, 2026). And even where a business is being surfaced, that surfacing is a probability distribution, not a fixed state (Schulte, Bleeker & Kaufmann, 2026).

Taken together, these findings describe a Perception Clock that cannot move at the Structural Clock's speed, by construction. A wide G(t) in the early months of an AEO engagement is not a sign that the work failed. It is the default, predictable output of how these systems are built.

4.3 When the gap becomes a genuine signal

The gap stops being routine and starts being a real problem when it fails to narrow over an extended period despite sustained citation-building effort. Based on the trajectory in Section 5 and the mechanisms in Section 2, G(t) is typically widest around months one and a half to three, when structural work is essentially complete but AI recognition has barely moved — and it should show measurable narrowing by month six. If it hasn't narrowed at all by that point, the diagnostic questions are:

  1. Is the business actually accumulating third-party citations and mentions, or only publishing on its own site? Self-published content does not move the Perception Clock the way external validation does.
  2. Is this a category-wide pattern or a business-specific one? If competitors in the same space are equally unrecognised, the lag may simply reflect how slowly that category is covered by AI training and retrieval sources generally.
  3. Is there conflicting or negative information elsewhere online that a model may be weighting instead of, or alongside, the business's own material? This channel is mechanically real: Wu, Wu, and Zou (2024) show that when retrieved content conflicts with a model's internal prior, models defer to the retrieved material more than 60% of the time — even when their own prior was correct. Conflicting third-party material can genuinely displace what a model would otherwise say about a business.

A persistent gap that survives all three questions — external signal is accumulating, competitors are recognised, and no conflicting information exists — is the case in which the Two-Clock Model would treat the engagement itself as underperforming.

4.4 Testable predictions

The model is falsifiable. Stated as explicit predictions:

  1. P1 — No external signal, no narrowing. If structural work is completed but no third-party citations or mentions accumulate, G(t) should remain wide indefinitely: S(t) alone does not move P(t).
  2. P2 — Citation velocity leads perception. When the rate of external citation accumulation rises, P(t) should improve before — and without — any further structural change.
  3. P3 — Platform curves diverge. Because platforms differ in retrieval design and update cadence (M4) and cite distinct source sets (Yang, 2025), the same business should show different perception curves on different platforms; a single blended score should mask this.
  4. P4 — Retrieval-grounded platforms move first. For entities created after a model's training cutoff, early perception gains should appear first on retrieval-grounded platforms, and only later — after retraining — in parametric knowledge.

Any sustained observation to the contrary — most sharply, a well-resourced citation-building program that leaves P(t) flat while competitors' recognition moves — is evidence against the model as stated, not merely an unlucky case.

5. Illustrative Case: Client Zero

AISearch Global launched on 20 May 2026 and used its own brand as Client Zero — running the exact AEO Traction Stack it sells, on itself, from day one. The case is one illustration of the framework, not evidence for it: a single engagement, with no counterfactual, whose perception timeline is itself a draw from a noisy process (Schulte, Bleeker & Kaufmann, 2026). Full method and measurement detail — instruments, prompt design, model versions, and the observed score table — appear in Appendix A.

The observed window is short — at the time of writing (5 July 2026) the engagement is in its seventh week — and the observed pattern within it is the one the model predicts. S(t) moved from 19 at launch to 70 at one month (20 June 2026) and stands at 91 as at 5 July 2026: a 72-point observed gain in under seven weeks, the Structural Clock behaving exactly as Section 2.6 predicts a well-executed structural program should. Perception scores, measured once on 20 June 2026 (one month post-launch), stood at 36 (ChatGPT-4o), 30 (Perplexity), and 41 (Gemini 1.5 Pro; mean ≈ 36) — low, which is itself expected: the launch postdates the training cutoffs of every model measured, so there was no citation history to draw on. Figure 4 plots the two curves. Everything to the right of the 5 July 2026 line is a modelled projection, not measured data — the business has existed for less than two months, and Client Zero is currently sitting at the start of the widest-gap window the model predicts (months 1.5–3). The value of the case is the observed shape so far — fast, early structural gains and a wide Perception Gap that has not yet had time to close — and the projection is the model's falsifiable forecast of what the next ten months should look like (Section 4.4).

6. Closing the Gap: Three Levers

The Perception Clock cannot be forced to run faster directly — there is no setting that makes a model re-learn a business sooner. The point holds even under far more access than any business will ever have: Cohen et al. (2024) show that purpose-built knowledge-editing methods, applied directly to a model's weights, fail to propagate even a single updated fact consistently through the model's related knowledge. If direct editing can't reliably re-teach a model one fact about an entity, no external party can force the update from outside. The clock can only be influenced indirectly, by giving AI platforms more high-quality material to find, evaluate, and reference. Three levers do this, and each maps onto a specific mechanism from Section 2.

6.1 Citation building (targets M4)

Getting referenced by external sources AI platforms already trust — directories, industry publications, review platforms — gives retrieval indexes fresh, authoritative material to surface the next time they refresh. This addresses the retrieval-staleness mechanism in Section 2.4 directly: it doesn't make the index refresh faster, but it improves what's waiting to be picked up when it does. The work is also platform-specific: analysing more than 366,000 citations across OpenAI, Perplexity, and Google search products, Yang (2025) finds that citations concentrate heavily among a small set of outlets and that different providers cite distinct sources. The set of sources worth appearing in differs by engine — which is also why perception should be tracked per platform rather than as one blended score.

6.2 External mentions (targets M5)

Independent third-party coverage is a stronger perception signal than anything a business publishes about itself. This is the most direct countermeasure to the long-tail knowledge problem in Section 2.5: a business becomes less long-tail, in a model's terms, in proportion to how much other, independent sources are saying about it. Models weight what others say about a business more heavily than what it says about itself. This lever has near-direct empirical support: Kandpal et al.'s (2023) finding that entity-level accuracy scales with the number of documents mentioning the entity is, mechanically, a statement that accumulated external mentions are what move an entity out of the long tail.

6.3 Answer-format content (applies the Section 2.6 evidence)

Content written to directly answer the specific questions a business's customers actually ask gets picked up and cited more readily than generic marketing copy. This is the practical application of the GEO findings in Section 2.6 — structuring content the way Aggarwal et al.'s benchmark shows measurably improves inclusion in generated answers. Field data sharpens the target formats: in Kumar's (2026) citation panel, ranked "best-of" listicles are the single most-cited content type, at roughly 21% of all citations, while about 78% of citations overall go to corporate websites — owned content in the right format does get cited. This is precisely what the Citation Consistency layer of the AEO Traction Stack is built for — the layer that starts the Perception Clock in earnest, rather than only optimising the page itself.

7. General Discussion

The Client Zero case in Section 5 behaves exactly as the theoretical model in Sections 2–4 predicts: fast, direct structural movement, and a Perception Clock that has not yet had time to respond. The remainder of this section draws out what that agreement implies theoretically, practically, and for how AEO/GEO outcomes should be measured going forward.

7.1 Theoretical Implications

The Two-Clock Model contributes a unifying frame to what is currently a scattered literature. Knowledge-cutoff research (Cheng et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024), temporal-fact-decay research (Zhang & Choi, 2023), retrieval-system design (Vu et al., 2023; R. Li et al., 2026; S. Li et al., 2026), long-tail knowledge representation (Kandpal et al., 2023; Mallen et al., 2023; Badhe, Shah, & Kathrotia, 2026), knowledge editing (Cohen et al., 2024), and generative engine optimisation (Aggarwal et al., 2024; Kumar, 2026) have, until now, been treated as separate research questions with separate practical implications. Reframed through the Two-Clock Model, they resolve into predictions about a single underlying variable — the rate at which an AI system's stated knowledge of an entity converges with that entity's current, real-world state — observed from two different vantage points (what a business controls, and what the platform controls). This reframing is falsifiable in principle; Section 4.4 states the predictions explicitly.

Nothing in mechanisms M1–M5, moreover, is specific to businesses. The same two clocks govern how AI systems update what they say about universities, governments, products, books, researchers, and public figures: structural readiness moves at the speed of the entity's own publishing, while AI perception moves at the speed of training cycles, retrieval refresh, and third-party corroboration. Stated at that level, the Two-Clock Model is a model of AI knowledge propagation; AEO is its first application, not its boundary.

7.2 Practical Implications

For agencies: sell and report on two workstreams, not one. A single AEO score, presented alone, sets a client up to expect a linear result the underlying systems cannot produce. Reporting S(t) and P(t) side by side, with G(t) explained as a mechanism rather than a mystery, is what separates a structural-fix vendor from a genuine AEO practice.

For businesses: measure both clocks, expect the gap to be widest early, and treat premature abandonment as the largest single cause of wasted AEO spend. Walking away at month two, because the structural score looks finished and AI recognition doesn't yet reflect it, cuts the engagement off exactly when the citation-building work that closes the gap is meant to begin.

7.3 Implications for Measurement

Schulte, Bleeker, and Kaufmann's (2026) finding that cited-source overlap runs as low as 32–43% across repeated identical queries (Figure 2) means a single perception check, at a single point in time, is not a reliable read. Any claim about where a business stands on AI recognition should be based on repeated measurement across time and across prompts, not one dashboard snapshot — the same discipline this paper's own method (Appendix A) applies, by running each prompt three times per platform rather than once. Yang's (2025) finding that different providers cite distinct sources adds the second measurement rule: track perception per platform, not as one blended number.

8. Limitations

Three limitations bound the claims in this paper. First, the Client Zero data in Section 5 is a single illustrative case, not a controlled study; no counterfactual is available, and the timelines it shows should not be read as a guarantee for any other business, market, or competitive set.

Second, the research cited in Section 2 studies model behaviour at a general level. Publicly benchmarked, mechanism-level findings about any single production consumer AI product — ChatGPT, Gemini, or Perplexity specifically, at any given moment — are far scarcer, and those platforms continue to change their training and retrieval systems on their own timelines.

Third, the specific figures cited here will keep shifting as models and measurement methods evolve; the underlying structural claim — that two independent clocks exist and move at different speeds — is what this paper argues holds regardless of which figures are current.

9. Conclusion

An AEO score and an AI's actual recognition of a business are answers to two different questions, produced by two different systems, on two different timelines. The Structural Clock is fast, direct, and fully within a business's control. The Perception Clock is slow, indirect, and governed by training cycles, retrieval refresh schedules, and citation accumulation that belong to the platforms, not the business. The space between them — the Perception Gap, G(t) = S(t) − P(t) — is not a sign the work has failed. It is the normal, well-evidenced relationship between these two clocks in the early months of any AEO engagement, and it closes through sustained citation building, external mentions, and answer-format content, not through more schema.

Appendix B. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Two-Clock Model in AEO?

It describes two parallel timelines in AEO work. The Structural Clock measures how machine-readable a site is and moves fast, in days or weeks, under the business's direct control. The Perception Clock measures what AI platforms actually know and cite about the business, governed by training cycles and retrieval refresh schedules, and moves slowly, often over months, outside the business's control.

Why did our AEO score improve but AI still doesn't recognise us?

This is the expected result of the Perception Gap, not a failure. Structural work moves the fast clock. AI platforms only update what they know about a business on their own training and retrieval cycles — the slow clock — which lags behind even after structural work is complete.

Can the Perception Clock be sped up directly?

Not directly. There is no setting that makes a model re-learn a business faster. It can only be influenced indirectly, through citable content and accumulating external mentions over time.

Is a gap between AEO score and AI recognition a sign something is broken?

No. A gap between a high structural score and low AI recognition is the normal state early in AEO work. It only signals a genuine problem if it fails to narrow over an extended period — roughly six months — despite sustained citation-building effort.

Why does a single AI-visibility check sometimes contradict a check done a week earlier?

Because AI search answers are probabilistic. Research on repeated identical queries (Schulte, Bleeker & Kaufmann, 2026) found cited-source overlap of only 32–43% between runs, and day-to-day brand-mention overlap of 45–59%. A single check is a sample from a distribution, not a fixed reading — which is why perception should be tracked over repeated measurements, not one snapshot.
